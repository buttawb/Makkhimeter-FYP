Why flies?

Fruit flies have been widely used in scientific research for over a century. They are easy to breed and maintain in the laboratory, have a short lifespan, and produce a large number of offspring. Additionally, fruit flies share many genetic similarities with humans, making them an ideal model organism for genetic research. In fact, 75% of the genes that cause disease in humans can be found in fruit flies. With our web application, you can explore the genetic makeup of fruit flies and study their traits, mutations, and more. Start your research today!

Life span

Fruit flies have a relatively short lifespan of about 8-14 days, which can vary depending on the environmental temperature. Despite their small size of 3mm, they are easily visible without a microscope. Their simple and rapid reproductive cycle allows for several generations to be observed within a matter of months, making them an ideal subject for research.

Reproduction

Reproduction in Drosophila is remarkably efficient. In just a matter of weeks, a single pair of flies can produce hundreds of offspring. These offspring mature sexually within a mere week, showcasing the species' impressive rate of growth and development (Lutz 1948). As with other insect species, Drosophila melanogaster lays eggs which subsequently hatch into larvae (maggots) that consume the fruit upon which they were laid (Patterson and Stone 1952).
